### Recap of Lecture 9 — High-Altitude Take-Home Points

- Physiological **responses** and **adaptations** to high-altitude hypoxia occur along the **entire oxygen supply cascade** — no single step is rate-limiting in isolation.
- **Metabolic adaptations** increase anaerobic energy supply, enhance mitochondrial efficiency, and minimize oxygen demand.
- **Movement efficiency** (e.g., flight biomechanics in geese, slow climbing in summiteers) is itself a hypoxia strategy because it lowers the energy that must be supplied aerobically.

![Slide titled "Humans living at high altitudes: Do high altitude natives exhibit adaptations in max ventilation rates?" with diagrams of the human thorax and a scatter plot of change in thoracic volume per breath versus tidal volume (L) for different groups: Quechua people (indigenous of South America) born and raised at high altitude, born at low altitude and tested at high altitude, and lowland controls. ### Tidal Volume Capacity in Highland Natives

- A study of **Quechua** indigenous people of South America addressed an open question from the previous lecture: do high-altitude natives have **higher maximum ventilation**?
- Answer: **yes** — Quechua individuals have **larger thoracic volume capacity** and reach **higher tidal volumes** than lowlanders.
- Comparing those born and raised at high altitude vs. born at low altitude and moved to high altitude vs. lifelong lowlanders revealed:
  - Higher tidal volume occurs **regardless of birth or testing altitude** — suggesting a **genetic** component
  - But individuals from hypoxic environments have **even greater** tidal volume capacity, indicating additional **developmental plasticity**.

## Slide 4

![Slide titled "Challenges of pregnancy at high elevations" with a figure caption "Fetal environment is naturally hypoxic (Everest in utero)." A small sketch of a pregnant woman is at left. A graph at right shows oxyhemoglobin saturation (%) versus oxygen partial pressure for adult hemoglobin (red curve) and fetal hemoglobin (blue curve, leftward shifted). Bullet points note: high altitudes impair fetal growth (limited blood flow to placenta); low birth weight and impaired brain development; high infant mortality at high altitudes; indigenous mothers have higher placental blood flow than ancestry of European populations at similar altitudes; in high altitudes, babies of indigenous mothers have higher birth weights than babies of European ancestry.](images/lec10/slide-004.png)

### Pregnancy and Childbirth at High Altitude

- The **fetal environment is naturally hypoxic** — even at sea level it has been likened to "Everest in utero." **Fetal hemoglobin** is leftward-shifted relative to adult hemoglobin to enable O<sub>2</sub> uptake from maternal blood.
- At high altitude, the homeostatic responses that protect the **mother** (e.g., systemic vasoconstriction) **restrict placental blood flow**, causing:
  - **Low birth weight**
  - **Impaired brain development**
  - High **infant mortality**
- **Indigenous high-altitude populations** show evolved compensations:
  - Higher placental blood flow
  - Higher birth weights at altitude than babies of European ancestry
- Historical observation: when European colonizers first arrived in Andean mining regions, **no child of European descent survived birth for many decades**, while indigenous infant survival was much higher.

## Slide 5

![Slide titled "Adaptations for high-altitude in highlanders" with a photo of researcher Cynthia Beall, summarizing comparisons of Tibetan, Andean, and lowlander populations. Bullet points note that both groups are highland-adapted but converged on different solutions; Tibetans have higher ventilation and circulatory flow (high cardiac output and high ventilation) but remain hypoxemic; Andeans have higher hemoglobin concentration and higher blood oxygen saturation but lower ventilation rates. A "Why the differences?" section notes the oxygen supply cascade is complex; mutations occur at random in populations; trade-offs exist between cardiac output and red blood cell count because higher viscosity limits cardiac work; low birth weight and infant mortality are key selection pressures, and each population converged on solutions that enhance infant survival. Citation: Beall 2007.](images/lec10/slide-005.png)

### Why Tibetans and Andeans Converged on Different Solutions

- **Trade-off** at the heart of high-altitude adaptation: increasing **cardiac output** and **[Hb]** simultaneously raises blood **viscosity**, increasing the work of pumping and limiting cardiac output.
- **Tibetans** solve the problem with **high ventilation and high cardiac output** — and remain mildly hypoxemic.
- **Andeans** solve it with **high [Hb] and high arterial saturation** — and use lower ventilation rates.
- Why two different solutions?
  - The cascade has many tunable steps
  - Mutations arise randomly, so different founding populations had different starting variation
  - Strong **selection pressure** comes from **infant mortality**: both higher placental blood flow and higher fetal blood oxygenation independently improve survival, but the two cannot be combined without raising viscosity-related risks.

| Adaptation | Time scale |
|------------|------------|
| Ventilation, hematocrit | **1–14 days** |
| Metabolic adaptations | **14–21 days** |
| Athletic performance plateau | **~20 days** |

- Variation across studies reflects differences in training regimens and individual responses.

## Slide 10

![Slide titled "Adaptations for diving in mammals" with the Berenbrink cartoon and a bullet list summary: diving involves repeated exposure to acute hypoxia with breath holding; vertebrates initially evolved and radiated in aquatic environments with relatively low O2; high O2 in air has enabled multiple lineages of land-living tetrapods to independently evolve high metabolic rates (endothermy, high aerobic scope); multiple secondary land-to-water transitions have occurred among endothermic vertebrates (whales, seals, etc.); water is too oxygen-poor to support the high metabolic rates of these endotherms, so all return to the surface to breathe air; aquatic mammals rely on intermittent surfacing to breathe air. Citation: Berenbrink 2021.](images/lec10/slide-010.png)

### Why Diving Is an Acute-Hypoxia Problem

- Vertebrates first evolved in **aquatic** environments, then **tetrapods** diversified on land where high atmospheric O<sub>2</sub> supported the evolution of **endothermy** and **high aerobic scope**.
- When mammalian lineages **secondarily returned to water**, they did **not** revert to gills — water cannot support the metabolic demands of an athletic endotherm.
- Diving mammals therefore **surface intermittently** to breathe, and dives are episodes of **acute, repeated hypoxia** with completely shut-down alveolar ventilation.

## Slide 11

![Slide titled "Factors in gas transport" showing a labeled diagram of the cardiopulmonary circuit (lungs, heart, vasculature) and a list of factors: alveolar ventilation, partial pressure gradients, diffusion conductance, ventilation-perfusion matching, O2-hemoglobin binding, convective blood O2 transport, O2-myoglobin binding, myoglobin store in muscle, capillary distribution in muscle, mitochondrial distribution in muscle.](images/lec10/slide-011.png)

### Which Cascade Steps Matter for Diving?

- During a breath-hold dive, **alveolar ventilation is shut off** — the diver depends entirely on **stored O<sub>2</sub>** distributed across the rest of the cascade.
- Critical factors during a dive:
  - **O<sub>2</sub>-hemoglobin** binding and total blood O<sub>2</sub> store
  - **Myoglobin** binding and muscle O<sub>2</sub> store
  - **Capillary distribution** in muscle (regulating where blood flow goes)
  - **Mitochondrial distribution** (matching diffusion distance)

## Slide 12

![Slide titled "Myoglobin acts as an oxygen store in muscle" with two oxygen dissociation curves on the same axes: hemoglobin (sigmoid curve, right-shifted) and myoglobin (hyperbolic, leftward-shifted, near-saturation at low PO2). Bullet points: myoglobin binds O2 at low PO2; very low PO2 at the metabolic sink in tissues → shuttles O2 from capillaries to mitochondria; acts as O2 store in muscle → buffers muscle O2 demand at exercise onset.](images/lec10/slide-012.png)

### Myoglobin: The Intramuscular O<sub>2</sub> Store

- **Myoglobin (Mb)** is a single-subunit O<sub>2</sub>-binding protein in muscle cells, with much **higher O<sub>2</sub> affinity** than hemoglobin (its dissociation curve is hyperbolic and **leftward shifted**).
- Functions:
  - **Shuttle** O<sub>2</sub> from capillaries to mitochondria across the very low intracellular P<sub>O₂</sub>.
  - Act as an **intramuscular O<sub>2</sub> store** that buffers the start of exercise and, in divers, the duration of breath holds.

![Slide titled "Adaptations for diving in mammals" with a Weddell seal photo and a graph of hemoglobin concentration ([Hb], g/dL) versus time (minutes) during a dive. [Hb] rises sharply for the first ~10 minutes of diving and then returns to baseline within 12–16 minutes after surfacing. Bullet points: [Hb] and [RBC] increase for the first 10 minutes of diving; return to resting values in 12–16 minutes post-dive; the Weddell seal has the largest spleen as a fraction of body mass of any mammal (~7% body mass); estimated 60% of the RBC mass is stored in the spleen.](images/lec10/slide-015.png)

### The Spleen as an On-Demand O<sub>2</sub> Reservoir

- **[Hb] rises rapidly during a dive** in Weddell seals, then returns to baseline 12–16 minutes after surfacing.
- Mechanism: **systemic peripheral vasoconstriction** during the dive squeezes the **spleen**, **injecting stored red blood cells** into circulation.
- The Weddell seal has the **largest spleen** as a fraction of body weight of any mammal (~7% of body mass).
- An estimated **60% of total RBC mass is stored in the spleen at rest**, available on demand for diving.

### Dynamic Spleen Function

- The **spleen functions dynamically** as a contractile O<sub>2</sub> reservoir:
  - Contracts on dive onset → **injects red blood cells** into the circulation
  - Refills during the recovery interval between dives
- This allows the seal to **defend C<sub>aO₂</sub>** for an extended period despite the absence of pulmonary gas exchange.
- The combination of large blood volume + concentrated erythrocyte reservoir is a **distinctive cardiovascular** adaptation of long-duration divers.

## Slide 17

![Slide titled "Adaptations for diving in mammals" featuring the equation $C_aO_2 = 1.39 \cdot [Hb] \cdot s_{O_2} + P_aO_2 \cdot 0.03$, with a definition list: 1.39 = grams O2 bound per gram Hb at full saturation; sO2 = fractional saturation of Hb with O2; PaO2 = partial pressure of O2 in arterial blood; 0.03 = solubility of O2 in blood. A photo of a free-diving seal is at left, and a scatter plot at right shows arterial oxygen content vs. dive depth or time across species.](images/lec10/slide-017.png)

### Quantifying Blood O<sub>2</sub> Content for Divers

- Arterial O<sub>2</sub> content is given by:

$$C_aO_2 = 1.39 \cdot [Hb] \cdot s_{O_2} + 0.03 \cdot P_aO_2$$

- Where:
  - **1.39** mL O<sub>2</sub>/g Hb = O<sub>2</sub> binding capacity at full saturation
  - **s<sub>O₂</sub>** = fractional saturation of hemoglobin
  - **P<sub>aO₂</sub>** = arterial O<sub>2</sub> partial pressure
  - **0.03** = O<sub>2</sub> solubility coefficient (small contribution from dissolved O<sub>2</sub>)
- For divers, **increasing [Hb]** (via splenic release) is the dominant lever for raising C<sub>aO₂</sub>, since s<sub>O₂</sub> cannot be raised above ~100%.

## Slide 18

![Slide titled "Reduced muscle capillary density in seals" with two histology micrographs comparing skeletal muscle from a typical mammal (more capillaries) and a seal (fewer capillaries, more myoglobin). Bullet points: in diving, vasoconstriction reduces convective oxygen transport, with hemoglobin concentrating; harbor and Weddell seals have ~60% capillary density of typical terrestrial mammals; vasoconstriction, anatomical shunt that reduces convective oxygen transport to muscle, while maintaining supply to vital organs and brain; mitochondria are distributed uniformly through the muscle tissue (in contrast to the dense distribution at fiber boundaries in distance to Mb-O2); no consistent differences observed in Hb or Mb binding affinity curves among diving-adapted species. Citation: Davis 2014.](images/lec10/slide-018.png)

### Reduced Capillary Density and Anatomic Shunting

- Counterintuitive at first: diving mammals have **lower** muscle capillary density (~60% of typical mammals).
- The combination of **systemic peripheral vasoconstriction + low capillary density** acts as an **anatomical shunt**:
  - **Reduces convective O<sub>2</sub> transport** to muscle
  - **Preserves O<sub>2</sub> delivery to vital organs** (brain, heart)
- The **ischemic muscle environment** drops local P<sub>O₂</sub> to where the steep portion of the Hb dissociation curve dumps a much larger fraction of bound O<sub>2</sub> into the tissue.
- **Mitochondria are distributed uniformly** through the muscle to minimize diffusion distance to myoglobin.
- Notably, **Hb and Mb binding affinity curves are not consistently shifted** in diving species — the regulation is in **vascular control**, not in the binding proteins themselves.

- Across mammals, **muscle [Mb] correlates with myoglobin net surface charge (Z<sub>Mb</sub>)**.
- Higher net positive charge enables **higher [Mb]** in muscle without aggregation.
- Sperm whales and other deep divers have **much higher Z<sub>Mb</sub>** than terrestrial mammals like pigs.

## Slide 20

![Slide titled "Adaptations for diving in mammals" with the same Z_Mb scatter plot from the previous slide and additional bullet points: high concentration of myoglobin in whale muscle; enabled by high charge that prevents aggregation by charge repulsion. Photos compare meat color in pig, cattle, and whale muscle — whale muscle is much darker red, reflecting higher myoglobin content. Citation: Berenbrink 2021.](images/lec10/slide-020.png)

### Why Charge Allows Higher Myoglobin Concentrations

- A higher **net surface charge** causes Mb molecules to **electrostatically repel** each other, preventing aggregation at very high intracellular concentrations.
- This permits **whale muscle** to carry far more Mb per gram than pig or cattle muscle, visible as a much **darker red** color.
- This is a clear example of a **molecular-level** adaptation enabling a **macroscopic** physiological capability.

## Slide 25

![Summary text slide titled "Adaptations for oxygen delivery and exercise in acute hypoxia during diving" with bullet points: blood and muscle tissue oxygen stores are critical for performance in acute hypoxia; systemic peripheral vasoconstriction reduces convective oxygen transport at active muscle and enables effective use of blood and muscle oxygen stores; vasoconstriction in the spleen → injects RBC O2 reservoir into circulatory system; large spleen size increases blood oxygen storage capacity to prolong dive duration; adaptations in myoglobin surface charge enable higher amount of oxygen concentration in muscle tissue and increased muscular store of oxygen.](images/lec10/slide-025.png)

### Lecture 10 — Key Takeaways

1. **Diving is acute hypoxia**: alveolar ventilation is shut off, so divers depend on **stored O<sub>2</sub>** in blood and muscle. Pulmonary gas exchange and inspired P<sub>O₂</sub> are temporarily irrelevant during the dive itself.
2. **Diving has evolved independently** in many mammalian lineages, and the convergent adaptations point to which steps of the cascade are most critical for breath-hold performance.
3. **Myoglobin** is the dominant intramuscular O<sub>2</sub> store; its leftward-shifted dissociation curve makes it a perfect short-term reservoir near mitochondria.
4. **O<sub>2</sub>-store distribution** differs sharply between humans (mostly lung) and specialized divers (mostly **blood and muscle**); humans can sustain BMR only for ~2 minutes from internal stores.
5. **The spleen acts as an RBC reservoir** in Weddell seals (~7% body mass) and, on a smaller but real scale, in **Bajau** human divers (PDE10A genetic association).
6. **Systemic peripheral vasoconstriction** + **reduced muscle capillary density** create an **anatomical shunt** that preserves O<sub>2</sub> for the brain and heart and maximally exploits the steep portion of the Hb dissociation curve to dump O<sub>2</sub> at active tissue.
7. **High-charge myoglobin** evolved independently in cetaceans and pinnipeds, allowing extreme intramuscular [Mb] without aggregation — a molecular adaptation behind the **dark red muscle** of deep divers.
8. **Hb and Mb binding curves themselves are not shifted** in most diving species — adaptation is in **vascular regulation, total store, and protein concentration**, not in O<sub>2</sub> affinity.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Acute hypoxia** | A short-duration episode of reduced O<sub>2</sub> availability — in diving, caused by breath-holding rather than by reduced inspired P<sub>O₂</sub>. |
| **Breath-hold diving** | Diving without external air supply; the diver depends entirely on stored O<sub>2</sub> for the duration of the dive. |
| **Myoglobin (Mb)** | Single-subunit O<sub>2</sub>-binding protein in muscle cells with high affinity and a hyperbolic (leftward-shifted) dissociation curve; functions as both an intramuscular shuttle and an O<sub>2</sub> store. |
| **Myoglobin net surface charge (Z<sub>Mb</sub>)** | The total electrostatic charge on the myoglobin surface; high values prevent aggregation at high concentrations and have evolved independently in cetaceans and pinnipeds. |
| **Splenic O<sub>2</sub> reservoir** | The store of red blood cells in the spleen; mobilized at dive onset by vasoconstriction. ~60% of RBC mass is stored splenically in Weddell seals, which have spleens up to ~7% of body mass. |
| **Systemic peripheral vasoconstriction (dive response)** | Reflex constriction of arterioles supplying skeletal muscle and non-essential organs at dive onset; preserves O<sub>2</sub> delivery to brain and heart. |
| **Anatomical shunt (in divers)** | The functional redirection of blood away from muscle by reduced capillary density and vasoconstriction; reduces convective O<sub>2</sub> transport to muscle while preserving central organ perfusion. |
| **Bajau ("Sea Nomads")** | An indigenous Southeast Asian people whose subsistence breath-hold diving has been associated with enlarged spleens and a PDE10A genetic variant. |
| **PDE10A** | A gene whose variants are associated with larger spleen size in the Bajau, mediated by effects on thyroid hormone signaling. |
| **Weddell seal** | A model species for diving physiology; long-duration diver with the largest spleen as a fraction of body mass of any mammal (~7%). |
| **Mass-specific O<sub>2</sub> stores** | Total body O<sub>2</sub> store per unit body mass, partitioned into lung, blood, and muscle compartments; specialized divers have much higher blood and muscle fractions than humans. |
| **Cross-current gas exchange** | The avian lung arrangement (perpendicular blood and air flow) — intermediate in efficiency between countercurrent (fish gill) and tidal-pool (mammalian lung) exchange. Reviewed at the start of this lecture for context. |
| **Countercurrent gas exchange** | Arrangement in which blood and the respiratory medium flow in opposite directions, maximizing diffusion gradient across the entire exchange surface. Found in fish gills and various heat-exchange systems. |
| **Tidal-pool exchange** | The mammalian lung arrangement: gas in the alveolus does not flow directionally relative to blood, so the gradient equilibrates more rapidly than in countercurrent or cross-current systems. |
